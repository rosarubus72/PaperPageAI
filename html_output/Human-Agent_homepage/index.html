Abstract

In the quest for scientific progress, communicating research is as vital as the discovery itself. Yet, researchers are often sidetracked by the manual, repetitive chore of building project webpages to make their dense papers accessible. While automation has tackled static slides and posters, the dynamic, interactive nature of webpages has remained an unaddressed challenge. To bridge this gap, we reframe the problem, arguing that the solution lies not in a single command, but in a collaborative, hierarchical process. We introduce AutoPage , a novel multiagent system that embodies this philosophy. AutoPage deconstructs paper-to-page creation into a coarse-to-fine pipeline from narrative planning to multimodal content generation and interactive rendering. To combat AI hallucination, dedicated "Checker" agents verify each step against the source paper, while optional human checkpoints ensure the final product aligns perfectly with the author's vision, transforming the system from a mere tool into a powerful collaborative assistant. To rigorously validate our approach, we also construct PageBench , the first benchmark for this new task. Experiments show AutoPage not only generates highquality, visually appealing pages but does so with remarkable efficiency in under 15 minutes for less than $0.1. Code and dataset will be released at Webpage 1 .

Motivation

The primary motivation for this research stems from the limitations of existing methods in automating the generation of project webpages for academic papers. Traditional approaches, such as converting papers into static visual formats like slides or posters, fall short when applied to interactive, dynamic webpages due to their rigid, non-flexible nature and inability to handle complex, varied paper structures. Additionally, current agent-based systems, though capable of generating high-quality static content, are inadequate for interactive webpages that require detailed narrative planning, multimodal content generation, and interactive rendering. This mismatch necessitates a fundamentally new approach that can manage the inherent complexity of webpage creation through a hierarchical, coarse-to-fine generation process augmented by human-agent collaboration. Addressing this gap is crucial because it frees researchers from the time-consuming and repetitive task of manually creating project webpages, allowing them to focus on core research activities. Furthermore, ensuring factual accuracy and visual appeal in automated webpages is challenging due to the risk of AI hallucination, which highlights the need for robust quality control mechanisms. Thus, this research aims to develop a multi-agent system, AutoPage, that not only generates high-quality, interactive webpages but also ensures alignment with the author's vision through iterative human oversight.

Overview of AutoPage. AutoPage conducts a multi-agent pipeline for transforming papers into interactive webpages: (1) Narrative Planning and Structuring parses PDFs into Markdown and generates section-level outlines; (2) Multimodal Content Generation produces coherent text-visual sections; (3) Interactive Page Rendering matches templates, compiles full HTML pages, and performs final layout checks. Throughout all phases, AutoPage integrates verification mechanisms and optional human-in-the-loop checkpoints for reliable and flexible generation.

Innovation

The main innovations and key contributions of this paper lie in the introduction of a novel approach to automating the generation of high-quality, interactive project webpages for academic papers. Unlike previous works that primarily focused on converting papers into static visual formats like slides, posters, and videos, this paper addresses the unique challenges posed by project webpages, which require a flexible, scrollable structure and interactive elements. The proposed AutoPage framework introduces a hierarchical, coarse-to-fine generation process that incorporates iterative human-agent collaboration, ensuring both content accuracy and visual quality. By leveraging a multi-agent system, AutoPage decomposes the complex task into narrative planning, multimodal content generation, and interactive page rendering, with each phase concluding with a verification step to mitigate AI hallucination. Additionally, AutoPage is designed to support flexible collaboration, allowing for human oversight to align the final output with the author’s vision. This approach not only enhances the performance of various large language models (LLMs) but also narrows the performance gap between different backbone models, making it a versatile and effective solution. The construction of PageBench, the first benchmark for this task, further validates the effectiveness of AutoPage and provides a robust framework for future research.

Methodology

The proposed method, AutoPage, addresses the challenge of creating dynamic, interactive project webpages from research papers through a hierarchical, multiagent system. At its core, AutoPage operates via a coarse-to-fine pipeline, starting with narrative planning and structuring. Initially, the source paper is parsed into a structured narrative blueprint using a combination of tools and Language Model (LLM) processing. This blueprint outlines the paper's content and visual elements, organizing them into a semantically rich asset library. Next, the Page Content Planner designs the webpage's high-level structure, optimizing the narrative flow for web presentation. This blueprint is verified for completeness and logical soundness before content generation. The system then integrates content modules into a chosen template, applying interactive features and ensuring visual integrity through automated checks. Users have the option to provide final adjustments through a Human-in-the-Loop mechanism, enhancing control and flexibility. To ensure the system's effectiveness, AutoPage avoids information leakage during evaluation by cross-pairing templates and content sources. The method is benchmarked against various LLMs, and experiments demonstrate AutoPage's capability to generate high-quality, visually appealing webpages efficiently. This approach transforms the process of creating project webpages from a manual, time-consuming task into a streamlined, collaborative effort, significantly enhancing researcher productivity and accessibility.

Experiments

The experiments conducted included benchmarking AutoPage against a variety of advanced language models, both closed-source (GPT-4o-mini, Grok-4-fast, GLM-4-Air) and open-source (Qwen3235B, GPT-OSS-120B, Llama-3.1-70B). AutoPage consistently demonstrated superior performance, particularly in generating webpages with higher aesthetic quality, better layout and cohesion, and improved semantic fidelity. In a user study involving 20 participants, AutoPage achieved the highest average score of 7.16, outperforming other models like Grok4-fast (6.93) and Gemini2.5-Flash (6.79). The study highlighted AutoPage’s effectiveness in producing webpages that are more coherent, dynamic, and accessible, as evidenced by higher user preference scores. Additionally, AutoPage was found to enhance the performance of various backbone models, narrowing the gap between high-performing and weaker models. This indicates that AutoPage not only excels in augmenting strong models but also significantly elevates the capabilities of weaker ones, thereby broadening the applicability of the framework. Overall, these results demonstrate that AutoPage is a robust and versatile tool for transforming academic papers into high-quality, interactive webpages, effectively lowering the barriers to scientific communication.
